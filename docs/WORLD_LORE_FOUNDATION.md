# World Lore Foundation — War Gods: Total War

This document defines the spine of the world so the simulation feels like history rather than a random fantasy generator. It is written for a low-fantasy medieval observer simulation where the database is the truth, the chronicle is interpretation, and historians may later disagree about what really happened.

The setting is designed for long runs of 1000+ years: cultures merge and split, religions fracture, provinces change names, dynasties die, exiles return, cities rise, empires rot, and the same war can be remembered as liberation, conquest, heresy, or betrayal depending on who writes the record.

---

## 0. Fixed Design Pillars

### Genre
Low fantasy medieval historical simulation.

Magic exists only at the edge of certainty. It is not a spellcasting economy, not a school system, and not something common people can rely on. Most people experience the supernatural through religion, omens, battlefield cults, curses, relics, dreams, disease, ruins, and stories that may or may not be true.

### Core Feel
The world should feel like:

- **Total War** for armies, sieges, roads, supply, ports, chokepoints, conquest and collapse.
- **Crusader Kings** for dynasties, legitimacy, succession, marriage politics, claims and betrayal.
- **Dwarf Fortress Legends** for historical memory, strange deaths, forgotten rulers, and generated chronicles.
- **Game of Thrones** for grounded politics and houses that are neither cleanly good nor evil.
- **Elden Ring history**, lightly, for the sense that ancient power shaped the world but is now half-ruined, misunderstood and fought over.
- **Mount & Blade** for regional warlords, unstable borders, mercenary companies, caravans and frontier violence.

### Player Role
The player is primarily an observer. The player watches history unfold through dashboards, chronicle reports, maps, ledgers, rumours, biographies, war reports and historian accounts.

Later systems may allow indirect influence, but never direct god-mode control. The world should not wait for the player.

### Tone
Brutal, grounded, political, historical, and occasionally legendary.

The writing can become poetic when describing deaths, oaths, disasters, old ruins, and religious moments, but the actual systems underneath must stay material: grain, iron, roads, marriage, debt, winter, disease, morale, manpower, legitimacy, terrain, supply and fear.

### Main Hook
A continent built over the corpse of an old empire, where war gods are worshipped, denied, exploited and feared. Kingdoms fight over roads, bloodlines, grain, iron, black salt, sacred battlefields and the right to define history.

### One-Sentence World Summary
**Auster is a battered medieval continent built on the corpse of the Veyran Dominion, where hungry kingdoms, oath-bound houses, temple armies, horse clans and port leagues fight for grain, roads, iron, black salt, and the right to claim that history itself was blessed by the war gods.**

## 1. Core World Identity

### 1.1 Fantasy Category
The world is **low fantasy** with dark historical edges. It should not feel like high magic, anime fantasy, Tolkien fantasy, or a clean heroic saga.

### 1.2 Gods
The gods are **real enough to matter, distant enough to argue over**.

No one can reliably prove that the war gods personally intervene. But omens, impossible survivals, cursed battlefields, madness after oath-breaking, and relic traditions make people believe. The simulation should never fully confirm or deny the gods unless a later design phase deliberately adds hidden metaphysics.

### 1.3 Magic
Magic is rare, feared and mostly religious.

Acceptable forms:

- battle omens
- saintly relics
- cursed ruins
- oath rituals
- battlefield madness
- black salt corruption
- prophetic dreams
- holy wounds that never heal
- old imperial machines or ritual sites that people mistake for divine power

Avoid:

- fireball mages
- magic schools
- common spellcasters
- mana crystals as a normal economy
- magical artillery
- chosen-one bloodline powers as a central mechanic

### 1.4 Creatures
The world is **human-first**.

There are no elves, dwarves, orcs, beastfolk, friendly fantasy races, monster adventurer guilds or dragon-riding empires.

Possible edge cases:

- wolves, bears, plague dogs and war beasts
- deformed animals around cursed lands
- supposed spirits in forests, marshes and battlefields
- old bones people call giants or dragons
- cults wearing demon masks
- rumours of undead soldiers after massacres

These should remain ambiguous unless the sim later adds a hidden supernatural layer.

### 1.5 End-State of a 1000-Year Simulation
By the end of a long run, the world should produce:

- rise and fall of empires
- dynastic drama
- succession wars
- religious transformation
- migrations
- cultural mixing
- city growth and city death
- lost roads and restored roads
- old claims turning into myths
- historians disagreeing about ancient events
- at least one attempted imperial restoration
- regions that feel permanently shaped by old trauma

The ideal output is not just who won. It is a readable history of why the world became what it became.

## 2. Era and Technology Level

### 2.1 Baseline Era
The default technology level is **high medieval with regional variation**.

Some regions are early medieval frontier societies. Some wealthy city-states are approaching late medieval sophistication. The old empire had better roads, bureaucracy, records and engineering than most current kingdoms.

### 2.2 Castles
Castles exist and matter.

Fortified hills, river forts, bridge castles, mountain keeps, port citadels and old imperial watchtowers should define military geography.

### 2.3 Armour
Mail, lamellar, scale, brigandine and partial plate exist. Full plate is rare, expensive and mostly limited to elite knights, royal guards and rich mercenary captains.

### 2.4 Gunpowder and Cannons
No standard gunpowder at start.

Black salt can create smoke, fire, ritual effects and limited siege terror, but it is not a reliable cannon economy yet. Late simulation technology may discover primitive bombards, but they should be unstable, expensive and politically disruptive.

### 2.5 Ships
Most ships are coastal, riverine, or central-sea capable. True oceanic exploration exists only as rare and dangerous expeditions.

Seas are both barriers and highways. Port control should matter as much as border forts.

### 2.6 Cities
Cities are rare and powerful.

A city should be a major political actor, not just a large province. A city can anchor trade, resist kings, hire mercenaries, mint coin, shelter claimants, finance coups, and survive by diplomacy.

### 2.7 Medicine
Medicine is practical but limited.

Herbalists, barber-surgeons, battlefield bone-setters, temple hospices and plague houses exist. Surgery is brutal. Infection is common. Religious medicine competes with practical medicine.

### 2.8 Farming
Farming is medieval and region-dependent.

Rich riverlands use mills, irrigation, crop rotation and manor systems. Frontier lands rely on hardy grains, goats, cattle, seasonal migration and raiding.

### 2.9 Literacy
Literacy is limited but not absent.

- Nobles: uneven, often trained in seals, titles and religious formulae.
- Priests and scribes: highly literate.
- Merchants: practical literacy is common in ports and city-states.
- Peasants: mostly oral culture.
- Steppe and mountain cultures: memory, oath-songs and marks can be more important than written law.

### 2.10 Armies
Standing armies are uncommon.

Most factions rely on levies, household troops, city militias, knightly retinues, tribal warbands, hired specialists and mercenary companies. Wealthy states may maintain professional guard regiments.

### 2.11 Mercenaries
Mercenary companies are important and dangerous.

They can win wars, bankrupt kingdoms, betray employers, seize forts, become landholding factions, or turn into religious military orders.

### 2.12 Cavalry
Cavalry is powerful but not universally dominant.

Heavy cavalry dominates open plains and rich noble warfare. Horse archers dominate steppe zones. Cavalry suffers in mountains, forests, marshes, winter, bad roads and during sieges.

### 2.13 Strategic Infrastructure
Roads, bridges and ports are major targets.

A faction that controls roads controls tax, movement, trade, armies and news. Old imperial roads should be treated as inherited strategic assets.

### 2.14 Sieges vs Field Battles
Sieges are more common than decisive battles.

Field battles can be decisive, but most wars should be won by supply, forts, betrayal, attrition, disease, river crossings and political collapse.

### 2.15 Movement Speed
Armies move slowly.

Movement should depend on:

- season
- roads
- terrain
- supply
- baggage
- weather
- commander skill
- local hostility
- cavalry ratio
- disease
- river crossings

Fast raids are possible. Large campaigns should feel heavy.

### 2.16 Disease and Winter
Disease and winter are deadly.

A strong army can die without losing a battle. Long campaigns should risk camp fever, famine, desertion, frostbite, horse loss and morale collapse.

### 2.17 Technology Progression
Technology can progress, stagnate or be lost.

Over 1000 years, cultures may discover better farming, shipbuilding, siege engineering, steelworking, record-keeping, coinage, medicine and black-salt applications. Civil collapse can destroy knowledge.

## 3. Geography and Map Shape

### 3.1 Main Map
The main map is one large known-world continent: **Auster**.

Auster should be large enough to support kingdoms, steppe migrations, sacred wars, imperial restoration, port leagues, isolated mountain cultures and frontier rebellions.

### 3.2 Sea
Auster has a central sea called the **Spear Sea**.

It acts like a highway, trade engine, naval battlefield and religious corridor. Whoever controls its ports controls grain, salt, coin, pilgrims, mercenaries and news.

### 3.3 Mountain Spine
A major mountain spine cuts across the continent: **the Marrowspine**.

It divides cultures, shelters old fortresses, hides mines, creates hard borders, and allows small mountain powers to survive against larger states.

### 3.4 Major River System
The primary river system is **the Veyr Basin**, a rich network of rivers, mills, towns and old imperial roads.

The Veyr Basin is the most contested heartland because it feeds armies and legitimizes kings.

### 3.5 Deserts
The south-east contains salt deserts and dry plateaus called **the Glass Wastes**.

They are poor in grain but rich in salt, black salt, incense routes and old buried cities.

### 3.6 Frozen North
The north contains harsh forests, iron hills, bogs and frozen marches called **the Frostmark**.

It is poor, militarized and hard to conquer.

### 3.7 Jungle/Rainforest
Dense southern forests exist but should not dominate the setting.

The **Green Teeth** are humid, disease-heavy, river-choked forests full of timber, medicinal plants, rebel sanctuaries and ruined shrines.

### 3.8 Steppe
The east contains horse grasslands called **the Savaan Reach**.

This is the source of horse clans, migration pressure, mounted warfare and border-raiding cultures.

### 3.9 Old Imperial Roads
Old Veyran roads still exist.

Some are intact stone highways. Some are broken causeways, drowned bridges, weed-choked marching roads or roads that now define borders.

### 3.10 Richest Lands
The richest lands are:

1. Veyr Basin riverlands
2. Spear Sea ports
3. western grain plains
4. southern spice and salt routes
5. mountain silver and iron corridors

### 3.11 Poorest But Hardest Lands to Conquer
The hardest lands are:

1. Frostmark northern marches
2. Marrowspine mountain valleys
3. Glass Waste salt settlements
4. Green Teeth forest interior
5. Savaan horse steppe

These regions are poor in tax but expensive to hold.

### 3.12 Trade Chokepoints
Major chokepoints:

- Spear Sea straits
- Veyr river fords
- Marrowspine passes
- old imperial bridge-cities
- salt-road caravan gates
- northern iron passes
- harbor chains and lighthouse forts

### 3.13 Sacred Places
Sacred sites are often battlefields, tombs, oath-stones, old imperial temples, river sources and mountain passes where war gods are believed to have judged armies.

### 3.14 Cursed/Ruined Places
Cursed places are old massacre fields, black salt pits, plague cities, failed imperial capitals, abandoned forts, drowned temples and roads where entire armies vanished.

### 3.15 Borders
Borders are mixed.

Natural borders exist along rivers, mountains, deserts and forests. Political borders come from old imperial provinces, marriage claims, conquest lines and religious jurisdictions.

### 3.16 Province Scale
Provinces should be **military-administrative regions** rather than tiny villages.

Each province should have:

- local_name
- imperial_name
- common_name
- terrain
- climate
- dominant culture
- dominant religion
- resources
- fortification level
- road/port/river importance
- unrest factors
- old trauma tag

### 3.17 Province Count
Initial target: **300–500 provinces**.

Start smaller for development, but the world design should support expansion. 100–200 is acceptable for early prototypes. 800+ should be a late ambition only after performance is proven.

### 3.18 Unknown Lands
The main continent is mostly known at start, but far coasts, deep forests, distant islands and desert interiors can be discovered, rediscovered or mythologized.

## 4. Major Cultures

Cultures should not be clean real-world copies. They can take broad inspiration from historical pressures, but their names, contradictions and systems should be unique to Auster.

### 4.1 Veyric Culture

**Location:** Veyr Basin, old imperial cities, central roads.

**Type:** Imperial, river, city-based, bureaucratic, legalistic.

**Values:** Law, grain, inheritance, records, roads, titles and continuity.

**Self-name:** Veyrani.

**Outsider stereotype:** Proud clerks with swords; people who think a stamped document is stronger than a spear.

**Enemy insult:** Ink-kings, road leeches, soft-bellied heirs.

**Military style:** Heavy infantry, disciplined spearmen, engineers, siege crews, river transport, fortified supply depots.

**Government style:** Kingdoms, duchies, governorates and old imperial offices pretending to be ancient rights.

**Family structure:** Patrilineal noble houses, recorded marriages, inheritance contracts, recognized bastardy systems.

**Dynasties:** Extremely important. A bad ruler with the right blood can be considered more legitimate than a brilliant outsider.

**Women:** Noblewomen can wield serious political power as regents, heiresses, marriage brokers and temple patrons, but open battlefield command is controversial.

**Foreigners:** Tolerated if taxed, registered and useful.

**Conquered peoples:** Integrated through law, tax categories, hostage education and title grants.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Forging lineage records, breaking sworn surrender terms, burning granaries, killing envoys, desecrating road shrines.

**Prestige:** Old seals, clean legal descent, stone villas, river estates, trained scribes, imperial titles.

**Names:** Controlled, formal, two-part names. Examples: Marrec Veyr, Calen Orvast, Sella Merun, Davic Halor.

**Place names:** Often preserve imperial names beneath common names.

**Language inspiration:** Crisp consonants, old administrative sound, restrained names. Avoid random Latin.

**Internal contradiction:** They worship law and continuity, but their nobles constantly bend old law through privilege, loopholes and forged ancestry.

---

### 4.2 Tharnic Culture

**Location:** Frostmark, northern iron hills, border castles.

**Type:** Mountain-north, frontier, clan-feudal, militarized.

**Values:** Survival, oath, iron, endurance, revenge and hard rule.

**Self-name:** Tharn.

**Outsider stereotype:** Cold, stubborn, violent and impossible to govern.

**Enemy insult:** Bog wolves, iron dogs, snow-eaters.

**Military style:** Axemen, shieldwalls, hill archers, winter raiders, heavy infantry, fortress garrisons.

**Government style:** Strongholds ruled by jarls, thanes, castellans and oath-kings.

**Family structure:** Clan households, fosterage, blood-debts and public oaths.

**Dynasties:** Important, but a weak heir can be challenged by a stronger oath-kin.

**Women:** Can hold forts, lead clans during war, manage blood-feuds and become oath-speakers. Direct battlefield command is accepted if proven.

**Foreigners:** Distrusted until they survive winter, fight honestly or marry in.

**Conquered peoples:** Often left under local elders if they pay iron, men and food.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Oath-breaking, guest-murder, cowardice during siege, selling clan land, abandoning kin in winter.

**Prestige:** Scars, iron rings, wolf cloaks, old grudges, frost-buried ancestors, grim songs.

**Names:** Short, hard, practical. Examples: Brann, Torvek, Halda, Sorn, Edrik, Vaska.

**Place names:** Harsh descriptive names: Ironford, Keld-Tharn, Blackmere, Snowgate.

**Language inspiration:** Northern, hard consonants, clan sounds. Avoid Tolkien dwarf energy.

**Internal contradiction:** They praise freedom and oath, but clan obligation can trap people harder than any king.

---

### 4.3 Savaan Culture

**Location:** Eastern grasslands, horse rivers, steppe borderlands.

**Type:** Nomadic, horse-based, confederation culture.

**Values:** Freedom, mobility, horses, tribute, courage, memory and sky-oaths.

**Self-name:** Savaan.

**Outsider stereotype:** Raiders who cannot build and cannot be trusted.

**Enemy insult:** Grass thieves, saddle-born, oathless riders.

**Military style:** Horse archers, light cavalry, feigned retreats, deep raids, encirclement, seasonal warbands.

**Government style:** Clan councils, war-khans, temporary confederations, charismatic leaders.

**Family structure:** Extended tent-clans, bride alliances, hostage-fosterage, adoption of captives.

**Dynasties:** Important through remembered blood, but leadership must be proven through horses, raids and generosity.

**Women:** Often economically and politically important. Some clans accept female riders, envoys and regents.

**Foreigners:** Useful as smiths, scribes, merchants, captives or spouses, but rarely trusted with herds.

**Conquered peoples:** Often taxed for grain, crafts and settlement goods rather than directly administered.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Horse theft from kin, betrayal under guest-right, poisoning wells, false surrender, killing children of hostage-bonds.

**Prestige:** Great herds, horse scars, eagle feathers, captured standards, generous feasts, remembered raids.

**Names:** Flowing but not ornate. Examples: Saran, Tulek, Arvan, Yessa, Koru, Mirak.

**Place names:** Named by water, grazing, old camps and graves: Blue Bend, Aruk Ford, Nine-Herd Plain.

**Language inspiration:** Steppe-like rhythm without direct copying.

**Internal contradiction:** They value freedom, but survival depends on strict clan discipline and harsh punishment for betrayal.

---

### 4.4 Irathi Culture

**Location:** Glass Wastes, salt roads, desert fortress towns.

**Type:** Desert, caravan, temple-merchant, survivalist.

**Values:** Water, contract, faith, salt, hospitality, secrets and endurance.

**Self-name:** Irath.

**Outsider stereotype:** Smooth liars, poisoners, money-priests and desert witches.

**Enemy insult:** Salt-bloods, sand-crows, oath-sellers.

**Military style:** Light infantry, camel troops, desert scouts, ambushes, fortified wells, hired crossbowmen.

**Government style:** Oasis councils, merchant houses, temple judges and hereditary water-wardens.

**Family structure:** Merchant families, water-right inheritance, alliance marriages, contract kinship.

**Dynasties:** Important in city-oases but less absolute among caravans.

**Women:** Often powerful in finance, water law, temple service and inheritance. Some cities allow female judges.

**Foreigners:** Welcome if they obey water law and pay tolls. Merciless toward those who steal water.

**Conquered peoples:** Usually absorbed through taxation, contract and conversion, not mass replacement.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Poisoning wells, breaking guest-water, counterfeiting salt weights, desecrating caravan dead.

**Prestige:** Silver water seals, fine glass, dyed veils, memorized contracts, clean wells, black salt rights.

**Names:** Dry, elegant, compact. Examples: Iram, Zahir, Nessa, Ruhan, Tavir, Selket.

**Place names:** Named after wells, salt, glass, heat and saints.

**Language inspiration:** Desert trade sounds, but not direct Arabic/Persian copy.

**Internal contradiction:** They treat hospitality as sacred, yet their politics are built on ruthless contracts, debt and controlled scarcity.

---

### 4.5 Myrric Culture

**Location:** Spear Sea ports, islands, ship cities and coastal leagues.

**Type:** Coastal, merchant, naval, urban.

**Values:** Trade, contracts, ships, reputation, information and city liberty.

**Self-name:** Myrr.

**Outsider stereotype:** Cowardly merchants who buy wars they refuse to fight.

**Enemy insult:** Coin gulls, harbor rats, tide-liars.

**Military style:** Marines, crossbow militias, naval raids, hired captains, blockade warfare, fortified harbors.

**Government style:** City republics, merchant councils, admiralty boards, elected war-captains.

**Family structure:** Merchant houses, apprenticeship ties, dowry networks, adoption for business succession.

**Dynasties:** Important as commercial houses rather than royal bloodlines.

**Women:** Often powerful as merchants, shipowners, guild heads and diplomats, depending on city law.

**Foreigners:** Welcomed, watched and priced.

**Conquered peoples:** Kept useful if ports remain profitable.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Burning ships in harbor, betraying convoy routes, debasing coin, violating safe-conduct, piracy against sworn league ships.

**Prestige:** Great ships, lighthouse patronage, rich ledgers, foreign spices, sea victories, debt owed by kings.

**Names:** Smooth, short, maritime. Examples: Lior, Mavek, Sella, Orin, Tessa, Joric.

**Place names:** Ports, capes, harbors, market districts, lighthouse names.

**Language inspiration:** Mediterranean port rhythm without obvious copy-paste.

**Internal contradiction:** They praise liberty, but poor debtors and foreign labourers can be trapped harder than serfs.

---

### 4.6 Kheldic Culture

**Location:** Marrowspine valleys, mines, high passes.

**Type:** Mountain, mining, clan-city, defensive.

**Values:** Stone, memory, craft, ancestors, independence and guarded wealth.

**Self-name:** Kheld.

**Outsider stereotype:** Miserly mountain hoarders and tunnel tyrants.

**Enemy insult:** Stone-mutes, goat-lords, pass-crows.

**Military style:** Pike blocks, slingers, mountain ambushes, fortress artillery, pass denial, mercenary engineers.

**Government style:** Valley councils, mine-lords, fortress abbots, clan compacts.

**Family structure:** Clan halls, craft lineages, ancestor debt and apprentice adoption.

**Dynasties:** Important in mine-rights and fort-rights, but councils can depose incompetent lineages.

**Women:** Often powerful in craft guilds, mine inheritance and religious memory keeping.

**Foreigners:** Allowed in lower towns, rarely trusted in high valleys or mines.

**Conquered peoples:** Usually made tributary rather than fully ruled.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Mine sabotage, false weight, betrayal of a pass, grave theft, selling clan maps.

**Prestige:** Masterwork weapons, carved ancestor stones, old mine charters, pass victories.

**Names:** Heavy but human. Examples: Keldan, Barvek, Orsha, Doran, Mikka, Tarn.

**Place names:** Stone, pass, hold, gate, peak, forge.

**Language inspiration:** Mountain-clan practicality without becoming dwarves.

**Internal contradiction:** They claim to hate kings, but mine-lords can become kings in everything but name.

---

### 4.7 Ossaric Culture

**Location:** Western grain plains, old frontier temples, cavalry estates.

**Type:** Agrarian, knightly, temple-feudal.

**Values:** Land, honour, harvest, cavalry, saints, marriage and rural legitimacy.

**Self-name:** Ossar.

**Outsider stereotype:** Proud horse-lords who think land makes them holy.

**Enemy insult:** Wheat-crowns, plough knights, soft saints.

**Military style:** Heavy cavalry, levy spearmen, household knights, banner warfare, seasonal campaigns.

**Government style:** Kingdoms, duchies, bishop-lords, knightly estates.

**Family structure:** Landed noble houses, peasant villages, temple marriage records, fostered squires.

**Dynasties:** Very important. Land without lineage is considered unstable.

**Women:** Powerful through inheritance, regency, abbeys and marriage politics, but formally restricted in many courts.

**Foreigners:** Accepted if Christian-like oath equivalent is sworn to local saints and harvest law.

**Conquered peoples:** Usually turned into tenants, serfs or oath-bound frontier settlers.

**Unforgivable crimes:** Kinslaying, church-burning, horse-maiming, harvest theft during famine, dishonouring hostage children.

**Prestige:** Warhorses, ancestral fields, saint banners, tournament scars, harvest feasts.

**Names:** Plain but noble. Examples: Alric, Marden, Oswin, Hella, Katrin, Daven.

**Place names:** Often named after fields, saints, mills, battles and founders.

**Language inspiration:** Western feudal sounds without direct English compound fantasy.

**Internal contradiction:** They preach honour, but marriage markets and inheritance politics make betrayal normal.

## 5. Religions and Belief Systems

### 5.1 The War Court

**Type:** Polytheistic war-god religion.

**Gods:** The war gods are worshipped as a court of powers rather than a loving pantheon. Each god represents a face of war: oath, siege, hunger, cavalry, sea war, vengeance, command, ruin and remembrance.

**Reality:** Ambiguous. Their influence is believed, feared and politically useful.

**Worldview:** The world is a battlefield where order is made through oath, sacrifice, courage and memory.

**Humans:** Humans are oath-bearers. A person is judged by what they defend, what they betray and what they endure.

**Afterlife:** The dead are taken into the Muster Beyond, forgotten in the Ash, or bound to the places where they broke their oaths.

**Sacred:** Battlefields, banners, oath-stones, commander tombs, weapons of famous dead, siege ruins.

**Sinful:** Cowardice, oath-breaking, false surrender, killing envoys, betraying guest-right, abandoning the dead.

**Rulers:** Must defend the land, punish treachery, honour oaths and win enough to prove divine favour.

**Commoners:** Must keep household oaths, pay lawful war dues, honour the dead and endure hardship.

**Clergy:** War-priests, banner-keepers, oath-speakers, shrine widows, battlefield grave-tenders.

**War:** Approved if oath-bound, defensive, revenge-based, sacred, or politically sanctified.

**Conquest:** Allowed if the conquered are given order and oaths.

**Serfdom/slavery:** Varies by sect. Some accept bondage of oath-breakers and captives; others condemn enslavement of the oath-innocent.

**Holy sites:** Great battlefields, old imperial victory arches, oath-stone hills, commander tombs.

**Religious law:** Strong in military matters and oath disputes.

**Heresies:** Yes. Sects can elevate one war god above the rest or deny the War Court entirely.

**Syncretism:** Very common with local ancestor worship.

**Political conversion:** Common. Rulers may convert to gain legitimacy, troops or temple recognition.

**Religious war trigger:** Desecration of a battlefield, killing priests under truce, denying an oath-sanctioned succession, banning war shrines, or claiming a rival people are oathless by nature.

---

### 5.2 The Hearth Dead

**Type:** Ancestor-based household religion.

**Reality:** Spirits are unproven, but ancestor rites shape family politics.

**Worldview:** The living are temporary caretakers of land, names and graves.

**Humans:** Humans are links in a chain of blood, adoption, debt and memory.

**Afterlife:** The dead remain near hearths, graves and ancestral stones if properly honoured.

**Sacred:** Graves, hearths, clan halls, birth trees, marriage cups, ancestor stones.

**Sinful:** Grave theft, name-erasure, kinslaying, refusing burial, selling ancestral land.

**Rulers:** Must protect graves, honour local elders and preserve inheritance order.

**Commoners:** Must maintain family rites and remember the names of the dead.

**Clergy:** Elders, grave-keepers, memory-women, clan singers.

**War:** Accepted for revenge, grave protection and land defence.

**Conquest:** Suspicious unless conquerors preserve graves and local households.

**Serfdom/slavery:** Captivity is less offensive than forced name-erasure.

**Holy sites:** Ancestor valleys, burial mounds, clan stones.

**Religious law:** Local and customary.

**Heresies:** Splits occur when adopted lines, bastards, exiles or conquered peoples claim ancestor rights.

**Syncretism:** Extremely common with the War Court.

**Conversion:** Rulers rarely fully convert away because ancestor legitimacy is political glue.

**Religious war trigger:** Grave desecration, forced renaming, exile of ancestral priests, or destruction of household records.

---

### 5.3 The River Covenant

**Type:** Legal-philosophical religion tied to old Veyran imperial order.

**Reality:** Less concerned with gods, more with cosmic law, water, grain and rightful order.

**Worldview:** Civilization exists because chaos is channelled like a river.

**Humans:** Humans are dangerous unless bound by law, duty and record.

**Afterlife:** The dead are weighed by whether they preserved order or broke it.

**Sacred:** Rivers, bridges, courts, granaries, contracts, census records, lawful roads.

**Sinful:** Fraud, unlawful rebellion, bridge burning, tax theft, grain hoarding during famine.

**Rulers:** Must uphold law, maintain roads, protect grain and submit to recognized rites.

**Commoners:** Must pay tax, obey law, maintain irrigation and respect courts.

**Clergy:** Judges, scribes, bridge-priests, river monks, archivists.

**War:** Approved when restoring lawful order.

**Conquest:** Approved if framed as reunification or pacification.

**Serfdom/slavery:** Legalistic; can justify harsh hierarchy if written into law.

**Holy sites:** Source temples, bridge courts, old imperial archives.

**Religious law:** Very strong.

**Heresies:** Yes. Reformers may argue the law belongs to all people, not nobles.

**Syncretism:** Common in old imperial cities.

**Conversion:** Very attractive to rulers seeking bureaucratic legitimacy.

**Religious war trigger:** Rival claimants disputing lawful succession, destruction of archives, refusal to recognize river courts.

---

### 5.4 The Salt Vow

**Type:** Desert contract religion.

**Reality:** Ambiguous. Its oaths are feared because oath-breakers often die in the desert, which believers treat as proof.

**Worldview:** Life exists by measured scarcity. Water, salt and word must never be wasted.

**Humans:** Humans are debt-bearers who survive through agreement.

**Afterlife:** The honest dead drink in the deep wells; oath-breakers wander thirsty.

**Sacred:** Wells, salt pans, caravan graves, black salt caves, sealed contracts.

**Sinful:** Water theft, false weights, contract betrayal, poisoning wells.

**Rulers:** Must protect wells, enforce weights and keep roads safe.

**Commoners:** Must honour guest-water, pay caravan dues and keep contracts.

**Clergy:** Well-judges, salt readers, caravan priests, oath witnesses.

**War:** Approved when punishing contract betrayal or defending wells.

**Conquest:** Accepted if new rulers preserve water law.

**Serfdom/slavery:** Debt bondage exists but is regulated by contract.

**Holy sites:** First Well, Dead Caravan Field, black salt shrines.

**Religious law:** Extremely strong in Irathi cities.

**Heresies:** Yes. Some sects reject debt bondage or claim black salt is holy corruption.

**Syncretism:** Common with the War Court among desert soldiers.

**Conversion:** Rulers convert for trade access and oath credibility.

**Religious war trigger:** Well poisoning, seizure of pilgrimage wells, or mass contract violation.

---

### 5.5 The Sea Ledger

**Type:** Maritime civic cult and trade faith.

**Reality:** Focuses less on gods and more on oaths, drowned dead, safe passage and reputation.

**Worldview:** The sea records every debt, betrayal and body.

**Humans:** Humans are names in a ledger of risk.

**Afterlife:** The drowned either reach the Deep Harbor or are claimed by storms.

**Sacred:** Lighthouses, ship bells, harbor chains, captain ledgers, drowned memorials.

**Sinful:** Mutiny without cause, false cargo logs, piracy under treaty, abandoning sailors.

**Rulers:** Must protect ports, enforce maritime law and honour convoy treaties.

**Commoners:** Must respect harbor dues, ship oaths and rescue customs.

**Clergy:** Harbor priests, ledger-keepers, storm widows, lighthouse orders.

**War:** Approved for blockade, anti-piracy and treaty enforcement.

**Conquest:** Disliked unless trade remains open.

**Serfdom/slavery:** Port cities may tolerate debt bondage while condemning illegal enslavement of sailors.

**Holy sites:** First Lighthouse, Drowned King Reef, Bell Harbor.

**Religious law:** Strong in maritime disputes.

**Heresies:** Yes. Pirate cults and storm sects can split away.

**Syncretism:** Common in every port.

**Conversion:** Rulers adopt rites to gain naval trust.

**Religious war trigger:** Harbor massacre, illegal blockade of sacred passage, execution of shipwrecked sailors.

## 6. The Old Empire

### 6.1 Name
The old empire was called **the Veyran Dominion**.

It should not be remembered the same way by everyone. To Veyric nobles, it was civilization. To many frontier cultures, it was tax, roads, hostages and crushed gods.

### 6.2 Founder
The official founder was **Marrec Veyr**, called the Road-King.

Some historians claim Marrec was a warlord who later had his crimes edited into statesmanship. Others claim there were three founders and Marrec became the convenient name.

### 6.3 Duration
The Dominion lasted roughly **470 years**.

### 6.4 Source of Power
The Dominion was powerful because it controlled:

- roads
- river grain
- census records
- forts
- tax law
- hostage education
- professional engineers
- standardized weights
- road shrines
- military colonies
- legal adoption of conquered elites

### 6.5 Public Memory
It is loved, hated, feared and mythologized depending on region.

- Veyric elites call it the Age of Roads.
- Tharnic clans call it the Chain Years.
- Myrric ports call it profitable but suffocating.
- Irathi traders remember treaty stability and tax cruelty.
- Savaan clans remember failed campaigns and hostage betrayals.
- Kheldic valleys remember mine seizures and road-building slavery.

### 6.6 What It Built
The Dominion built roads, bridges, forts, cities, canals, courts, grain stores, archives, census halls, temples, military colonies and border walls.

It did not create high magic systems. It created systems of control that later people mistake for supernatural order.

### 6.7 Collapse
The collapse was slow, then sudden.

Causes:

- succession crisis
- overextended borders
- provincial governors becoming hereditary rulers
- grain failures
- northern winter campaigns
- plague along roads
- port debt
- temple legitimacy crisis
- army pay collapse
- betrayal by frontier commanders
- black salt sickness in key mines

### 6.8 The Last Breaking
The remembered final crisis is called **the Year of Three Empty Thrones**.

Three claimants were crowned in three cities. Two died before harvest. The third ruled a road network nobody obeyed.

### 6.9 Time Since Fall
At the default campaign start, the Dominion fell around **180–220 years ago**.

This is long enough for memory to become myth, but recent enough for roads, laws, titles and grudges to survive.

### 6.10 Claimants to the Legacy
Potential heirs:

- Veyric kings with old blood claims
- River Covenant judges who claim law outlives blood
- military governors who hold old forts
- Myrric cities that preserved imperial coinage
- Ossaric cavalry houses married into imperial remnants
- pretenders with forged seals
- religious leaders who claim the war gods withdrew favour from the old line

### 6.11 Who Wants to Erase It
Groups that may want the Dominion forgotten:

- Tharnic independence clans
- Kheldic mine councils
- Savaan confederations
- peasant reform cults
- anti-tax city factions
- rulers whose legitimacy depends on local freedom, not imperial inheritance

### 6.12 Ruins
Ruins include:

- broken roads
- drowned bridges
- plague cities
- abandoned census halls
- fortress monasteries
- sealed archives
- black salt mines
- triumph arches
- old barracks turned into towns
- half-collapsed aqueducts

### 6.13 Surviving Laws and Titles
Surviving titles:

- Roadwarden
- Grain Prefect
- High Marshal
- River Judge
- Gate Duke
- Census Lord
- Bridge Count
- March Warden

These titles may now mean very different things depending on region.

### 6.14 Restoration
Rebuilding the Dominion is possible but morally complicated.

It could restore roads, peace and law. It could also recreate hostage-taking, tax cruelty, cultural suppression and military colonization.

No restoration should be cleanly good.

## 7. Why Factions Hate Each Other

Wars in Auster should have layered causes. A war should rarely be only about one thing.

### 7.1 Common War Causes

- land hunger
- claims and inheritance
- disputed marriages
- trade routes
- river tolls
- access to the Spear Sea
- old imperial succession
- sacred battlefields
- black salt rights
- horse pasture
- iron passes
- revenge killings
- border raids
- hostage deaths
- debt defaults
- mercenary betrayal
- religious legitimacy
- class revolt
- serf flight
- city autonomy
- nomadic migration

### 7.2 Blood Feuds
Blood feuds between houses should persist for generations.

A feud can start from:

- a murdered heir
- a broken betrothal
- a hostage killed in captivity
- a false accusation of bastardy
- a siege massacre
- a disputed river crossing
- an insult during funeral rites

### 7.3 Buffer States
Buffer states should be extremely important.

Examples:

- mountain pass duchies between river kingdoms and Kheldic valleys
- frontier marcher states between Veyric lands and Savaan clans
- port cities everyone needs but nobody trusts
- desert toll states controlling wells

### 7.4 Hated But Necessary Trade
Enemies should still trade when survival demands it.

Examples:

- Veyric river kingdoms need Savaan horses.
- Savaan clans need settled grain and ironwork.
- Tharnic forts need southern salt.
- Myrric ports need inland grain.
- Irathi cities need timber and metal.
- Kheldic mines need food imports.

This creates realistic tension: hatred does not cancel economics.

## 8. Resources That Actually Matter

### 8.1 Core Strategic Resources

1. **Grain** — the most important resource. Armies, cities and legitimacy depend on food.
2. **Iron** — required for weapons, armour, tools and fortification hardware.
3. **Horses** — strategically vital and unevenly distributed.
4. **Timber** — needed for ships, siege engines, housing and winter fuel.
5. **Stone** — needed for castles, bridges, walls and prestige buildings.
6. **Salt** — needed for preservation, trade, survival and military logistics.
7. **Silver/Coin Metal** — needed for mercenaries, diplomacy and city power.
8. **Black Salt** — the main fantasy resource.

### 8.2 Black Salt
Black salt is a rare mineral found in desert pits, cursed marshes, old battlefields and certain sealed mines.

It is used for:

- preserving bodies
- oath rituals
- medicine in dangerous doses
- smoke weapons
- siege terror
- temple rites
- embalming war heroes
- sealing documents
- battlefield sanctification
- strange fire experiments

### 8.3 Effects of Black Salt
Black salt affects war, religion and legitimacy.

A ruler who controls black salt can fund temples, sanctify campaigns and hire specialists. But black salt also causes sickness, hallucination, birth defects, mine madness, cult obsession and worker revolt.

### 8.4 Resource War Drivers

- Most wars: grain and roads.
- Bloodiest wars: inheritance and sacred sites.
- Most destabilizing wars: black salt and succession.
- Most common raids: horses, cattle and slaves.
- Most urban conflicts: tolls, ports, coin, debt and guild rights.
- Most frontier conflicts: pasture, timber, iron and forts.

### 8.5 Resource Depletion
Resource depletion can happen over centuries.

Forests can disappear. Mines can flood. Salt pans can become exhausted. Farmland can be ruined by war. Cities can collapse if roads shift.

Every important place should have history inside the name.

- resources

- Forts: `Hold`, `Watch`, `Stone`, `Gate`
- Rivers: `-run`, `-beck`, `-drift`

- Ports: rare, usually practical names.

#### Savaan
- Towns: seasonal camps, `-aruk`, `-saan`, `-tul`
- Forts: usually borrowed words from settled cultures.
- Rivers: `Blue`, `White`, `Horse`, `Reed`

- Ports: uncommon.

#### Irathi
- Towns: `-ir`, `-vash`, `-ket`, `-sul`
- Forts: `Kasr`, `Watch`, `Well-Fort`, `Salt Gate`

- Mountains: `Glass`, `Red`, `Dry`, `Knife`

#### Myrric
- Towns: `-port`, `-quay`, `-bell`, `-lios`
- Forts: `Chain`, `Tower`, `Light`, `Harborfort`
- Rivers: named by mouths and trade.
- Mountains: coastal capes and lookout names.
- Ports: central identity.
- Style: maritime, civic, practical.

#### Kheldic
- Towns: `-keld`, `-dorn`, `-bar`, `-mek`
- Forts: `Hold`, `Deep`, `Pass`, `Forge`
- Rivers: `Cold`, `Stone`, `Deep`, `Grey`
- Mountains: `Peak`, `Spine`, `Tooth`, `Anvil`
- Ports: not common.
- Style: stone, mine, pass and ancestor names.

#### Ossaric
- Towns: `-field`, `-mere`, `-ton`, `-wold`, `-wick`
- Forts: `Castle`, `Banner`, `Hall`, `Watch`
- Rivers: `-brook`, `-run`, `-wash`
- Mountains: usually named after saints, battles or lords.
- Ports: secondary.
- Style: rural, saintly, land-based.

### 9.5 Avoid in Names
Avoid:

- over-apostrophes
- joke names
- random Latin
- obvious Tolkien names
- obvious anime names
- overly English fantasy compounds like Stormblade, Shadowfang, Lightspire
- clean good/evil names
- names that sound like MMO zones

## 12. Political Structure

### 12.1 Faction Types
The world supports:

- kingdoms
- duchies
- marcher states
- city-states
- port leagues
- clan confederations
- oasis councils
- religious orders
- mercenary companies
- rebel factions
- governorates
- pretender courts
- warlord states
- restored empires

### 12.2 Political Rules

1. Factions are mostly kingdoms and regional powers at start.
2. Empires can exist but should be rare and unstable.
3. Duchies and marcher states are important buffers.
4. City-states are powerful through money, ports and diplomacy.
5. Tribes and clans are real political actors, not background flavour.
6. Religious orders can hold land and armies.
7. Mercenary companies can become landless factions or seize territory.
8. Rebels can become real factions if they control land and legitimacy.
9. Governors can become independent when central authority weakens.
10. Generals can found kingdoms after military collapse.
11. Merchants can buy titles in corrupt or indebted states.
12. Priests can crown, shame, sanction or help depose rulers.
13. Dynasties can hold multiple kingdoms through marriage and inheritance.
14. Marriage politics is central.
15. Legitimacy often matters more than raw military strength.
16. Factions can split peacefully through inheritance or councils.
17. Factions can collapse into warlord states.
18. Small factions can survive through diplomacy, terrain, trade or sacred status.
19. Cultures can exist without states.
20. A rightful ruler is one who combines blood, oath, recognition, protection, religion, law and practical control.

### 12.3 Legitimacy Ingredients
A ruler is considered rightful when enough of these are true:

- recognized bloodline
- accepted coronation rite
- control of capital or sacred site
- elite support
- military protection
- priestly approval
- legal claim
- marriage alliance
- victory in war
- ability to feed people
- respect for local customs
- possession of old imperial title or seal

Legitimacy should be a score, not a binary.

## 13. War and Military Identity

### 13.1 Frequency
Wars are frequent, but not all wars are total wars.

Most conflicts are raids, border campaigns, sieges, claim wars, punitive expeditions, caravan wars, succession conflicts and city blockades.

### 13.2 Battle Decisiveness
Battles matter, but should not always decide wars.

A battle can:

- kill a ruler
- destroy legitimacy
- break a levy host
- create a saint or martyr
- trigger rebellion
- bankrupt a faction
- shift alliances
- become a sacred memory

But sieges, supply and politics should often matter more.

### 13.3 Commanders
Commanders can die, be captured, betray, defect, gain fame, lose face, found factions or become hated by their own soldiers.

### 13.4 Armies
Armies can defect, mutiny, desert, starve, freeze, spread plague or turn into bandits.

### 13.5 Mercenaries
Mercenaries can betray employers if unpaid, mistreated, bribed or offered land.

### 13.6 Supply
Supply collapse can destroy stronger armies.

A large empire can lose a war because:

- roads are cut
- winter arrives
- local granaries are burned
- plague enters the camp
- mercenaries go unpaid
- a claimant rebellion starts at home
- river crossings fail
- horses die
- vassals refuse to answer
- the war loses religious legitimacy

### 13.7 Naval War
Naval war is important around the Spear Sea and major rivers.

Blockades, convoy attacks, port sieges, river crossings, pirate treaties and plague ships should all matter.

### 13.8 War Types
The simulation should support:

- border raids
- slave raids in harsher regions
- holy wars
- succession wars
- peasant wars
- civil wars
- mercenary wars
- punitive expeditions
- imperial restoration wars
- anti-tax revolts
- trade wars
- city league wars
- migration wars
- sacred-site wars

### 13.9 Elite Units
Elite units are rare.

They require resources, culture, training tradition, commander support and money. They should be vulnerable to attrition and politics.

### 13.10 Levies
Levies are unreliable.

They may fight well for homeland defence but collapse in distant campaigns, famine, disease or unpaid service.

## 14. Long-Term Simulation Systems

For 1000+ years, the following should be allowed.

### 14.1 Culture
Cultures can merge, split, assimilate, resist, revive and become court cultures.

A conquered province should not instantly become the conqueror’s culture. Culture change should require time, settlement, law, marriage, religion, prestige and economic pressure.

### 14.2 Religion
Religions can split into heresies, merge with local beliefs, become state faiths, lose influence, reform, radicalize or become tools of legitimacy.

### 14.3 Language and Names
Languages evolve. Place names change. Old names survive in local speech, chronicles, legal documents and ruins.

### 14.4 Titles and Claims
Titles can become obsolete. Old claims can expire, be forgotten, become myths or be revived by ambitious rulers.

### 14.5 Dynasties
Dynasties can die out, continue through women, be adopted, be forged, be restored from exile or survive only as a rumour.

### 14.6 Provinces and Cities
Provinces can depopulate. Cities can grow into capitals, collapse from plague, shift trade routes, become sacred, or be abandoned.

### 14.7 Capitals
Capitals can move because of conquest, legitimacy, trade, plague, religion or strategic necessity.

### 14.8 Trade
Trade routes can shift because of war, ports, roads, river silting, piracy, climate, plague or new technology.

### 14.9 Climate and Plague
Climate can change slowly. Plagues can reshape politics, labour, faith and migration.

### 14.10 Migration
Migrations can create new factions, destroy old borders, merge cultures and cause religious change.

### 14.11 Empire Restoration
Old empires can be restored, but restoration should require:

- enough territory
- capital/sacred site
- legitimacy
- administrative capacity
- elite support
- military strength
- religious recognition or replacement ideology

### 14.12 Historians Disagree
Historians can disagree about old events.

Every major event should eventually support multiple accounts:

- hidden truth
- public account
- faction account
- later historian account
- hostile account
- religious account

## 17. Seed Content Direction

The first proper world seed should not try to generate the whole continent.

Start with one pressure zone where all major systems touch:

**Recommended Start Region:** the Veyr Basin border zone between river kingdoms, northern iron marches, a Spear Sea port, a mountain pass, and an eastern horse frontier.

This lets the sim immediately test:

- grain politics
- road control
- succession claims
- old imperial legitimacy
- border raids
- mercenary hiring
- port trade
- iron supply
- religious disputes
- cultural tension
- historian disagreement
- small factions surviving through terrain and diplomacy

### Seed Faction Mix

1. A Veyric river kingdom claiming the old Dominion.
2. A Tharnic northern march kingdom controlling iron.
3. A Myrric port city league controlling trade.
4. A Kheldic mountain pass compact controlling mines.
5. A Savaan horse confederation raiding the frontier.
6. An Ossaric cavalry duchy with a marriage claim.
7. An Irathi caravan house or embassy enclave controlling black salt access.
8. A religious military order guarding a sacred battlefield.

This gives enough pressure for the world to produce history without needing player input.

## 18. Final Lore Rule

Every system should obey this rule:

**Nothing appears without roots.**

A kingdom needs land, food, legitimacy, people and enemies.
A war needs claims, fear, profit, revenge, religion or survival.
A name needs language, class, place or history.
A rebellion needs pressure, memory and opportunity.
A chronicle needs bias.
A myth needs time.

If the simulation follows that rule, the world will feel old, cruel, human and alive.
